\begin{abstract}
We study data-driven statistical arbitrage in U.S.\ equities by learning trading
policies directly from \emph{residual} return series. Residuals are extracted with
three families of factor models---IPCA, PCA, and the Fama--French five-factor
model---and a neural trading policy is trained end-to-end to maximize an
out-of-sample Sharpe ratio. We evaluate a convolution--transformer architecture
with a graph component (\texttt{CNNTransformer}) against feed-forward baselines
(\texttt{RawFFN}, \texttt{FourierFFN}, \texttt{OUFFN}). This report describes the
data, methodology, and empirical results, including risk-adjusted performance and
turnover across factor models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Classical statistical arbitrage exploits temporary mispricings in the residual
component of asset returns---the part left after systematic (factor) exposure is
removed. Rather than assuming a parametric mean-reverting process (e.g.\ an
Ornstein--Uhlenbeck model) and hand-tuning entry/exit thresholds, we follow the
\emph{Deep Learning Statistical Arbitrage} framework and learn the trading policy
directly from data. The policy maps a window of recent residual behavior to a
portfolio weight, and is optimized to maximize a risk-adjusted objective on
out-of-sample (OOS) data via rolling retraining.

\section{Data and Residual Construction}
The investable universe is defined by a market-capitalization cap proportion
($\texttt{cap\_proportion} = 0.01$). For each asset we form a residual time series
by removing common factors using one of three factor models:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{IPCA} --- Instrumented Principal Component Analysis (here $5$ factors),
  \item \textbf{PCA} --- rolling-window principal component analysis ($5$ factors),
  \item \textbf{Fama--French} --- the daily five-factor model.
\end{itemize}
Daily trading dates are aligned to the Fama--French research data over the OOS
period (1998--2017). When \texttt{use\_residual\_weights} is enabled, the residual
composition matrix is used to translate residual-space positions into asset-space
weights for realistic turnover and short-proportion accounting.

\section{Methodology}

We compare four trading-policy architectures, all consuming a lookback window of
preprocessed residuals (cumulative-sum normalized): \texttt{RawFFN} (feed-forward on
raw windows), \texttt{FourierFFN} (Fourier features), \texttt{OUFFN}
(Ornstein--Uhlenbeck-inspired layer), and \texttt{CNNTransformer}. The remainder of
this report focuses on the \texttt{CNNTransformer} family and, in particular, its
graph-augmented variant \texttt{CNNTransformer+GNN}.

\subsection{The \texttt{CNNTransformer+GNN} architecture}
The policy maps, for each trading day, the recent residual paths of all tradable
assets to a vector of portfolio weights. Let $N$ be the number of active residuals on
a given day and $L$ the lookback ($L=30$). Processing flows through four stages
(implemented in \texttt{models/CNNTransformer.py}).

\paragraph{(1) Input and preprocessing.} For each residual $i$ and day $t$ we form a
window of the \emph{cumulative} residual return over the last $L$ days
(\texttt{preprocess\_cumsum}): $x_{i,t} \in \mathbb{R}^{L}$. A boolean validity mask
selects residuals with no missing observation inside the window, so the cross-section
$N$ varies over time without inducing look-ahead bias.

\paragraph{(2) Temporal convolution (per residual).} Each window is passed,
independently across residuals, through a 1-D convolutional block: two causal
(left-zero-padded) \texttt{Conv1d} layers with ReLU and instance normalization, a
residual skip connection, mapping $1\!\to\!8$ channels (\texttt{filter\_numbers}
$=[1,8]$, \texttt{filter\_size}$=2$). Output: a feature map in $\mathbb{R}^{8\times L}$
per residual. This stage extracts local mean-reversion/momentum patterns in each
residual's own path.

\paragraph{(3) Graph layer (cross-sectional, the GNN extension).} At each time step
the convolved features of all valid residuals are treated as nodes of a
\emph{fully connected} graph. Each node is updated from the concatenation of its own
features and the \emph{mean of its neighbors'} features, followed by a linear map
$\mathbb{R}^{2\cdot 8}\!\to\!\mathbb{R}^{8}$ and ReLU, with masking so that padded
(invalid) residuals contribute nothing:
\begin{equation}
  h_i' \;=\; \mathrm{ReLU}\!\Big(W\,\big[\,h_i \,\Vert\, \tfrac{1}{|\mathcal{N}_i|}\!\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} h_j\,\big]\Big),
  \qquad \mathcal{N}_i = \{\text{all other valid residuals}\}.
\end{equation}
This is the only component that shares information \emph{across} the cross-section;
disabling it (\texttt{use\_gnn=False}) recovers the paper-baseline
\texttt{CNN+Transformer}. With the GNN enabled the per-node feature dimension becomes
\texttt{gnn\_hidden\_dim}$=8$ ($1$ layer).

\paragraph{(4) Transformer and output head.} The (per-residual) feature sequence over
the $L$ time steps is fed to a \texttt{TransformerEncoderLayer}
(\texttt{attention\_heads}$=4$, feed-forward width $=2\times$ model dimension, dropout
$0.25$) which models temporal dependencies via self-attention. The representation at
the last time step is passed through a linear head $\mathbb{R}^{d}\!\to\!\mathbb{R}$,
yielding one raw weight per residual. Raw weights are normalized to unit gross
exposure, $w_t \leftarrow w_t / \sum_i |w_{i,t}|$, giving the dollar-neutral-style
portfolio for day $t$.

\subsection{Forward pass: from residual windows to portfolio weights}
For a single trading day $t$, the policy turns the recent residual paths of all valid
assets into one portfolio. There is no label; the ``prediction'' \emph{is} the
portfolio. The four stages of $f_\theta$ map the dimensions as follows
(batched over the $N$ assets):
\[
\resizebox{0.95\textwidth}{!}{$\displaystyle
\begin{aligned}
\text{(1) windows} \quad & x_{\cdot,t} \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times L}
   && \text{cumulative-return window per asset} \\
\text{(2) CNN} \quad & \xrightarrow{\;\text{1-D conv, }1\to8\text{ ch}\;} H \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times 8\times L}
   && \text{local temporal features, per asset} \\
\text{(3) GNN} \quad & \xrightarrow{\;h_i \,\mapsto\, \mathrm{ReLU}(W[h_i \Vert \bar h_{\mathcal N_i}])\;} H' \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times 8\times L}
   && \text{cross-sectional mixing (this stage = the extension)} \\
\text{(4) Transformer + head} \quad & \xrightarrow{\;\text{self-attn over }L,\ \text{then linear}\;} \tilde w_{\cdot,t} \in \mathbb{R}^{N}
   && \text{one raw weight per asset}
\end{aligned}$}
\]
followed by gross-exposure normalization $w_{i,t} = \tilde w_{i,t} / \sum_j |\tilde w_{j,t}|$.
In stage (3), $\bar h_{\mathcal N_i}$ is the mean of all \emph{other} valid assets'
features; setting \texttt{use\_gnn=False} removes this stage entirely, so the baseline
weight depends only on asset $i$'s own window.

\subsection{Training objective}
Given a block of days, the network is trained to make the resulting return series
$\{r_t\}$ have the highest possible Sharpe ratio, by minimizing
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta) \;=\; -\,\frac{\operatorname{mean}_t(r_t)}{\operatorname{std}_t(r_t)},
  \qquad r_t = \sum_i w_{i,t}\,\epsilon_{i,t} \;-\; (\text{trading costs}).
\end{equation}
Gradients flow from this Sharpe loss all the way back through the weights, the
transformer, the GNN, and the convolution---hence \emph{end-to-end}.

\subsection{Rolling train/test loop}
To keep every evaluated day out-of-sample, training and testing slide forward in
blocks of $f=125$ days; a fresh model is trained on the preceding $L_{\text{tr}}=1000$
days for each block (Algorithm~\ref{alg:rolling}).

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Rolling out-of-sample evaluation of the policy}
\label{alg:rolling}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require residual matrix $\epsilon\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times N}$; $L_{\text{tr}}=1000$; $f=125$; $L=30$
\For{$k = 0,1,\dots,\lfloor (T-L_{\text{tr}})/f\rfloor$} \Comment{$31$ subperiods}
  \State $\text{Train}_k \gets \epsilon[\,kf \,:\, kf+L_{\text{tr}}\,]$
         \Comment{1000-day training block}
  \State $\text{Test}_k  \gets \epsilon[\,kf+L_{\text{tr}}-L \,:\, kf+L_{\text{tr}}+f\,]$
         \Comment{next 125 days (strictly later)}
  \State initialize fresh parameters $\theta_k$ \Comment{rolling retrain}
  \For{epoch $=1,\dots,100$} \Comment{train on $\text{Train}_k$}
    \For{each minibatch of $32$ days}
      \State $w \gets$ normalize$\big(f_{\theta_k}(\text{windows})\big)$;\quad
             $r_t \gets \sum_i w_{i,t}\,\epsilon_{i,t}$
      \State $\theta_k \gets \text{Adam step on } \mathcal{L}=-\operatorname{mean}(r)/\operatorname{std}(r)$
    \EndFor
  \EndFor
  \State save checkpoint $\theta_k$; run $f_{\theta_k}$ on $\text{Test}_k$ \Comment{out-of-sample}
  \State record OOS returns $r_t$, turnover, short proportion for this block
\EndFor
\State \Return concatenated OOS return series and metrics over all $3{,}781$ test days
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\noindent With $T=4781$ and $f=125$ this gives $31$ retrained subperiods
(checkpoints \texttt{subperiod0}--\texttt{subperiod30}). Crucially, $\text{Test}_k$
always lies strictly after $\text{Train}_k$, so no future information leaks into the
reported performance.

\subsection{Evaluation metrics}
For each (model, factor model) pair we report the annualized Sharpe ratio
(daily Sharpe $\times \sqrt{252}$), mean daily return, return standard deviation,
turnover, and short proportion.

\subsection{Discussion}
Three findings stand out from Table~\ref{tab:summary}, computed over $3{,}781$
out-of-sample trading days with zero transaction and holding costs and without
residual-weight adjustment (\texttt{use\_residual\_weights = False}).

\paragraph{Factor model matters most.} The choice of residual-generating factor
model dominates performance. \textbf{PCA} residuals deliver the strongest
risk-adjusted returns (annualized Sharpe $\approx 5.0$), followed by \textbf{IPCA}
($\approx 4.2$) and \textbf{Fama--French} ($\approx 3.1$). The PCA advantage comes
not from higher mean returns---IPCA actually has a marginally higher mean daily
return---but from substantially lower volatility, yielding a cleaner, more
mean-reverting residual signal for the policy to exploit.

\paragraph{The GNN extension does not help here.} Adding the graph component
(\texttt{CNN+Transformer+GNN}) slightly \emph{reduces} the Sharpe ratio for all
three factor models: PCA $5.01 \to 4.91$, IPCA $4.20 \to 3.90$, and Fama--French
$3.08 \to 2.97$. The differences are small and the GNN does modestly lower turnover
(e.g.\ IPCA $1.11 \to 1.02$), but on this universe and configuration the
cross-sectional graph information provides no out-of-sample edge over the plain
convolution--transformer policy. This is a useful negative result: the extra
parameters do not pay for themselves before costs.

\paragraph{Why might the GNN add little? (interpretation).} A plausible explanation
is that the factor model has already removed the dominant cross-sectional comovement
among assets: residuals are, by construction, the part of returns left \emph{after}
common factor structure is stripped out, and are therefore close to idiosyncratic
and only weakly related across the cross-section. If little exploitable
cross-sectional structure remains in the residual series, the GNN's message-passing
across residuals has correspondingly little to add over a policy that already models
each residual's own temporal dynamics. We stress that this is an interpretation, not
a demonstrated mechanism: the performance margin is small (and within plausible
seed-to-seed variation), the comparison is conducted only in the residual-space,
cost-free regime, and a single GNN configuration (a one-layer fully connected graph)
was tested. The cross-sectional benefit of a graph component could still emerge under
realistic position accounting (\texttt{use\_residual\_weights = True}), under
transaction costs, or with a richer graph (e.g.\ sector- or correlation-based edges).

\paragraph{Turnover and costs.} All policies trade aggressively, with average daily
turnover near $1.0$--$1.1$ (the book is effectively rebuilt each day) and short
proportions of $0.35$--$0.51$. Because these results assume zero transaction costs,
the headline Sharpe ratios are gross figures; the high turnover implies the
ranking could compress materially once realistic costs are introduced, which is the
natural next experiment.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented an end-to-end deep learning approach to statistical arbitrage on
equity residuals and benchmarked several neural trading-policy architectures across
IPCA, PCA, and Fama--French factor models under a rolling out-of-sample protocol.
